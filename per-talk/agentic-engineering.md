# Deep Analysis: Agentic Engineering: Working With AI, Not Just Using It
**Speaker:** Brendan O'Leary | **Company:** Kilo Code (ex-GitLab) | **Date:** 2026-04-07

## Core Beliefs

**The AI agent is a junior developer — not a tool, not a senior engineer, something in between.** O'Leary's central metaphor is explicit and deliberate: "Think about your AI agent as an energetic, enthusiastic, extremely well-read, often confidently wrong junior developer." This framing does enormous rhetorical work. It sets expectations (will make mistakes), establishes the human's role (director/manager), and prevents both over-trust and under-trust. It's the single most important sentence in the talk because it determines every downstream architectural decision he recommends. The metaphor is deliberately asymmetric — the agent has breadth but lacks judgment, speed but lacks context, confidence but lacks understanding. He is not saying AI is a collaborator in the egalitarian sense; he's saying it's a report.

**Context engineering is the defining skill of the agentic era.** He quotes Karpathy ("context engineering is a delicate art and science") and then spends more time on context management than any other single topic. His specific claims are precise and clinical: tokens are expensive, quality degrades past ~50% context capacity, bad context corrupts output irreversibly, and attempting to "steer back" a derailed agent within the same session is a losing strategy. This isn't generic advice — the 50% threshold is a very specific performance claim that suggests either internal benchmarking at Kilo or access to non-public model behavior data.

**The research-plan-implement loop is the antidote to "prompt and pray."** He is openly disdainful of the demo aesthetic of "prompt and a whole lot of code flying by," calling it out as something he explicitly banned from Kilo's own website when he joined. His prescribed workflow — research (understand the system), plan (explicit steps with verification strategy), implement (execute against the plan in a fresh session) — is essentially a waterfall apology dressed in agentic clothing. The irony is notable: the talk about the cutting edge of AI engineering prescribes the most traditional software engineering methodology possible. He knows this. He's arguing that the discipline matters *more* with AI, not less.

**Human thinking is the irreplaceable bottleneck.** He quotes Dex Horthy twice: "AI can't replace thinking. It can only amplify the thinking you've done — or the lack of thinking you haven't done." This is O'Leary's deepest conviction. The research and planning phases are where humans must invest their time. Implementation is the low-leverage phase you hand off. This inverts the typical AI narrative ("AI handles the hard parts!") into something more honest: AI handles the tedious parts, but the hard thinking — architectural reasoning, scope definition, verification strategy — remains human territory.

**He's skeptical of the "just use it" camp.** The opening is a challenge: "90% of engineers are using AI tools... most engineers can't [explain how they work with it]." He sees uncritical AI adoption — "auto-completing your way through the day" — as the dominant failure mode, not rejection. The enemy isn't AI skepticism; it's AI complacency.

## Pain Points

**Context window poisoning is the silent killer of agent quality.** This is the problem he returns to most urgently. The failure mode is specific: you start down a path with an agent, realize it's wrong, try to course-correct within the same session, and the agent keeps resurfacing abandoned ideas from the corrupted context. His prescription — "always start a new session once you realize things are kind of off the rails" — is effectively an admission that in-context recovery is unreliable. This is a significant architectural limitation he's treating as a given, not a bug to be fixed.

**MCP server bloat is a growing, underappreciated problem.** He spends disproportionate time warning about this. The argument: MCP servers became so popular that engineers enable them reflexively, but each one loads context into every turn. A Postgres MCP running during front-end work is actively harmful — not just wasted tokens, but potential confusion for the agent about what it's allowed to touch. This reveals a tension in the ecosystem: the MCP standard was designed for extensibility, but its success is creating the exact context pollution problem he's warning about.

**The "jump straight to implementation" trap.** He describes the default user behavior — "Hey, help me implement this feature" — as the root cause of most AI frustration. The LLM obliges with a flood of code, assumptions compound, and "you've put garbage in and gotten garbage out." He's describing a failure mode he's clearly observed at scale through Kilo's user base. The specificity of the complaint suggests this is the #1 support issue or churn driver.

**Technically correct, contextually wrong code.** This phrase — "they'll confidently write code that is technically correct and contextually wrong" — captures the failure mode that breaks trust with AI tools. The agent doesn't know about the architectural decision from three months ago. It writes something that passes tests but violates an implicit constraint. This is the pain point that can't be solved with better prompts alone — it requires institutional memory (agents.md, skills, rules) and human review.

**Reviewing agent output is itself a skill nobody is teaching.** The Comic Sans intern story is actually a parable about review failure. The interns delivered exactly what the spec said. The fault was in the context provided. He's implicitly arguing that code review for AI-generated code requires a different discipline than reviewing human code — you're not checking for skill gaps, you're checking for context gaps. The Git-as-first-PR-review workflow he describes is his attempt to systematize this.

## Architecture & Technical Patterns

**Mode-based agent personas (Ask / Architect / Code).** Kilo's three modes are designed to enforce the research-plan-implement loop at the tool level. Ask mode *cannot write files* — it can only chat and read. This is an architectural constraint deliberately preventing the "jump to implementation" anti-pattern. Architect mode is for planning. Code mode is for execution. The key insight: the mode system is a guardrail against human impulsiveness, not against agent limitations. The agent *could* do all three in one mode; the separation exists to force the human into disciplined phases.

**agents.md as the new README.** He positions agents.md as "quickly becoming the de facto standard" for project-level agent context — conventions, build commands, testing requirements, commit criteria. This is a significant claim: it implies a convergence across agent tools (Cursor, Copilot, Claude Code, Kilo) around a single file format for project-level instructions. If true, agents.md is becoming the `.gitignore` of the AI era — a project-level configuration file that every tool agrees to respect.

**skills.md as reusable agent playbooks.** Distinct from agents.md (always-on context), skills are on-demand workflows loaded per task. The examples — "making motion graphics," "compiling changelogs" — suggest these are parameterized, repeatable procedures. The architectural pattern is essentially functions vs. global variables: agents.md is the global scope, skills are callable procedures. This is a clean separation that most agent toolkits haven't formalized.

**Session isolation as a core primitive.** His strongest technical recommendation is to use separate sessions for research, planning, and implementation. Each session gets a clean context window. The handoff mechanism is a document — the research output becomes the input to the planning session, the plan becomes the input to the implementation session. This is essentially a batch processing architecture applied to AI interaction: discrete steps with serialized state, not a continuous conversation.

**Summarization as a session handoff protocol.** "Have that agent summarize where you're at... start a new session with just that right context." This is a specific technique for context compression between phases. The agent summarizes its own work, the human reviews and corrects the summary, and the corrected summary becomes the seed for the next session. The interesting detail: "AI is really great at writing prompts for AI." He's using the agent to generate optimized context for a future agent — meta-prompting as an engineering practice.

**Git worktrees for parallel agent isolation.** Mentioned in passing but architecturally significant: using git worktrees so multiple agents can work on separate branches simultaneously, with human-controlled merges. This is source control being repurposed as an agent coordination layer. It also implies a workflow where the human is a merge gate, not an inline reviewer.

**MCP as a double-edged context loader.** His MCP analysis is more nuanced than most: they're useful (GitHub for PR context, Context7 for current docs) but each one is a permanent context tax. The recommendation to disable unused MCPs per-session is essentially arguing for dynamic, task-aware context loading rather than static global configuration. This is a feature most agent tools don't support well yet.

**Internal API integration — the four-tier strategy.** For enterprise consumption of internal APIs: (1) use OpenAPI/Swagger specs directly, (2) convert to markdown for agents.md, (3) use reference URLs for frequently-changing specs, (4) build custom MCP servers for complex multi-system workflows. This progression — from static documentation to dynamic tooling — is a maturity model for API integration that suggests he's seen enterprises struggle with every level.

## Hidden Signals

**"I made a pronouncement that we would never have our website be just prompt and a whole lot of code flying by."** This is a direct shot at Cursor, Windsurf, and every coding agent that leads with spectacle. It's also a product positioning statement: Kilo is selling discipline and reliability, not speed and magic. The word "pronouncement" — used by someone with an engineering management background — signals a top-down cultural decision, not just a marketing choice.

**The "smaller, faster, or cheaper model for implementation" aside.** Buried in the planning section: once you have a detailed plan, "we can even use maybe a smaller, faster, or cheaper model to implement it because we've spent the time in the research and plan phase." This is a consequential economic claim. It means the heavy model (expensive, slow) is needed for research and planning, but implementation against a clear plan can use a lighter model. This is a cost optimization architecture — and it implies Kilo may be building toward model routing or tiered execution.

**"Openclaw and Kiloclaw" mentioned at the close.** This is a brief reference to open-source agent safety infrastructure. The name pattern (OpenClaw → KiloClaw) mirrors OpenAI's model-to-product naming, suggesting Kilo is building an open-source safety layer with a commercial variant. The emphasis on "very safe way to use OpenClaw agents" signals that enterprise security is the next competitive frontier for coding agents. He doesn't elaborate, which means this is either early or strategically under-wraps.

**The GitLab background is doing more work than he acknowledges.** His git-centric workflow — local commits as first-pass review, worktrees for parallel agents, branches as agent isolation boundaries — maps exactly to GitLab's merge request culture. He's not describing a generic best practice; he's describing a git-native worldview that treats source control as the coordination fabric for human-agent collaboration. Teams using different VCS workflows (trunk-based, monorepo with automated merges) may find this pattern less natural.

**The Comic Sans story is actually about spec quality, not agent quality.** The interns did nothing wrong. The specification was the problem. He's making a structural argument: if the input is bad, the output will faithfully reproduce the badness. This maps directly to his argument about AI agents, but the fact that he chose an intern story (not an AI story) to illustrate it reveals something: he believes the failure modes of AI agents are isomorphic to the failure modes of junior humans. The solutions are also isomorphic — better specs, better context, better review processes. He's not describing a new category of engineering; he's describing old engineering discipline applied to a new type of worker.

**"Part art and part science" is said three times.** This repetition signals genuine uncertainty about where the field is. The advice is largely heuristic — "one task per session," "keep an eye on your context meter," "start a new session when in doubt." These are rules of thumb, not formalized principles. The field hasn't produced reproducible, quantitative frameworks yet, and O'Leary is honest about that.

## What's NOT Said

**No discussion of evaluation or measurement.** He never mentions how to measure whether an agent's output is good, how to benchmark agent performance over time, or how to A/B test different prompting strategies. The entire workflow is qualitative — the human reviews, the human decides, the human's judgment is final. For someone arguing for "engineering" discipline, the absence of metrics is conspicuous.

**No mention of specific models or model selection.** The talk is entirely tool-agnostic and model-agnostic. He never names a specific LLM, never discusses model capabilities, never addresses the question of which model to use for which phase. This is deliberate (Kilo positions itself as model-agnostic) but it avoids the elephant in the room: model quality varies dramatically, and the research-plan-implement loop may not work equally well across all models.

**No discussion of autonomous agents or multi-agent systems.** For a talk titled "Agentic Engineering," there's zero mention of agents talking to agents, agent orchestration, or autonomous workflows. His agents are strictly human-in-the-loop, single-threaded, session-bound. The entire talk assumes one human directing one agent at a time. The parallel agents mention is about isolation, not coordination. This is a deliberately narrow definition of "agentic."

**No security or safety discussion beyond the OpenClaw name-drop.** No mention of prompt injection, data leakage, supply chain attacks through MCP servers, or the risks of giving agents file system and terminal access. For an enterprise-focused product leader (ex-GitLab), this absence is either strategic (don't scare people) or telling (security isn't a differentiator yet in his segment).

**No discussion of team dynamics or organizational change.** The entire talk is addressed to individual engineers. There's nothing about how teams should adopt these practices, how to do agent-assisted code review as a team, how to share agents.md across a team, or how to handle the junior/senior engineer divide when AI is the new junior. The "agentic engineering" framework is solitary.

**No economic analysis.** Armin's "30% time savings" is the only quantitative claim, and it's cited third-hand without methodology. There's no discussion of token costs, ROI calculation, when AI assistance is cost-effective vs. not, or how to justify AI tool spend to management. For an enterprise product, the absence of a business case framework is notable.

**No failure stories from Kilo users.** The Comic Sans story is from his pre-AI career. The only AI-specific failure mode described is generic (context poisoning), not specific to Kilo or its competitors. Either he's protecting user data or the real failure stories are too close to competitive weaknesses.

## Speaker's Worldview

O'Leary is a pragmatist who believes the AI revolution will be won through old-school engineering discipline, not novelty. He sees the current moment not as a break from tradition but as a return to it — the same management rigor you'd apply to a team of junior engineers, now applied to AI agents. His fundamental bet is that the companies and engineers who treat AI as a disciplined collaborator (with clear phases, clean context, explicit plans, and rigorous review) will outperform those chasing raw speed or autonomy. He is selling process maturity as competitive advantage, which is exactly what you'd expect from someone who spent years at GitLab selling devops maturity to enterprises.

## Key Quotes

1. **"Think about your AI agent as an energetic, enthusiastic, extremely well-read, often confidently wrong junior developer. [...] They'll confidently write code that is technically correct and contextually wrong."** — The defining metaphor of the talk. Encodes the entire philosophy in one image.

2. **"More context doesn't always mean better results. In fact, it can make the model actually dumber."** — A counterintuitive claim delivered as fact. The "dumber" phrasing is deliberate — he's not saying "slightly less accurate," he's saying the model degrades qualitatively. This is the technical foundation for every context management recommendation.

3. **"Always start a new session once you realize things are kind of off the rails. [...] AI is really great at writing prompts for AI."** — Two ideas packed into one recommendation: don't try to fix corrupted context, and use the agent itself to generate clean handoff prompts. The second idea is underappreciated and underexplored.

4. **"AI can't replace thinking. It can only amplify the thinking you've done — or the lack of thinking you haven't done."** — Attributed to Dex Horthy but clearly adopted as O'Leary's own conviction. The "or the lack" clause is the critical part — AI will amplify your laziness too.

5. **"The question isn't whether your team is using AI, they are. The question is whether you're getting the most out of it or you're just kind of auto-completing your way through the day."** — The opening challenge. Reframes the entire conversation from adoption to effectiveness, which is exactly the framing that serves a premium tool vendor.
